# Ads Structure

Most ads managers organize ad creation in three layers.

## Creating Ads

An advertiser can either:

- bring their own creative
- create creative with the help of the ads manager

That means the ads manager is not just a distribution tool. In many products, it is also a lightweight creative system.

## The Common 3-Level Structure

### 1. Campaign

This is where the business goal gets defined.

Example:

- Campaign name: `Nike Running Shoes - Sales`
- Goal: `Website conversions`
- Budget: `$10,000`

At this level, the core question is:

**What am I trying to achieve, and how much am I willing to spend?**

### 2. Ad Group / Ad Set

Within the same campaign, different audiences or delivery themes get separated out.

Example:

- Ad Group 1: `Marathon runners`
- Ad Group 2: `Gym beginners`
- Ad Group 3: `Women 25-34 in California`

This layer usually controls:

- audience targeting
- placements
- bids
- schedule

At this level, the core question is:

**Which type of users should this campaign be shown to?**

### 3. Ads / Creatives

This is the actual ad the user sees.

Example inside the `Marathon runners` ad group:

- Ad A: `Built for long-distance comfort`
- Ad B: `Run your fastest mile`
- Ad C: video showing endurance runners

This layer usually includes:

- video or image
- headline
- caption
- CTA

At this level, the core question is:

**What exactly is the user going to see?**

## Why This Structure Exists

This three-level setup is useful because it separates three different decisions:

- campaign = business goal and budget
- ad group = delivery strategy
- ad = message and creative

That separation lets an advertiser test different audiences and creatives without rebuilding the whole campaign every time.

It also lets the platform optimize more cleanly:

- budgets can be managed at one layer
- audience choices can be compared at another layer
- creative winners can emerge inside the same delivery bucket

## TikTok Example

For a TikTok campaign promoting a food-delivery app:

- Campaign: `App installs`
- Ad Group 1: `College students in LA`
- Ad Group 2: `Working professionals in SF`
- Ads inside Group 1: late-night cravings creatives
- Ads inside Group 2: lunch-in-10-minutes creatives

In TikTok's structure:

- Campaign = objective
- Ad Group = audience or delivery bucket
- Ad = message and creative

So the simplest mental model is:

- campaign defines the goal
- ad group defines who should see it
- ad defines what gets shown

## Where The Model Gets Messier

Real platforms add more complexity on top of this:

- shared budgets
- automated targeting expansion
- dynamic creative combinations
- placement-level controls
- multiple attribution windows

So the three-level structure is the foundation, not the whole system. It explains how intent is packaged before auction, ranking, and measurement take over.
