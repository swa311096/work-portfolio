# SPL Role Understanding

My current understanding is that the SPL sits between the frontier AI lab and the expert network, but not as a generic recruiter. The SPL sits at the point where a model weakness has to become a high-quality expert-data project.

The lifecycle looks like this:

- The AI lab defines what "better" means.
- The lab evaluates the current model.
- The lab identifies failure patterns.
- The SPL translates those failures into an expert-data project.
- The SPL sources the right experts, designs the workflow, manages quality, and keeps delivery on track.
- Experts create, review, or label data.
- The lab uses the data to train, fine-tune, or evaluate the model.
- The loop repeats if the model still fails.

In simple terms, the SPL owns this stage:

> Model weakness -> expert workflow -> high-quality data delivered to the lab

That distinction matters. The lab is not just buying access to people. It is buying better model performance through high-quality human judgment. The SPL is the person who makes that human-expert layer work at frontier-lab quality and speed.

For example, if an AI lab says, "our model is weak at complex backend debugging," that is not yet an executable project. The SPL has to turn that vague need into an operating plan:

- What kind of engineers do we need?
- What level of seniority?
- Should experts create tasks, review outputs, rank responses, or write reference solutions?
- What should the rubric measure?
- How do we check quality?
- How many examples do we need, and how fast can we deliver?
- What happens if experts disagree?
- How do we keep the client updated?

That is the core SPL role: converting an ambiguous model-improvement need into a reliable expert-data system.

## Core Capabilities

### Understanding The Model Improvement Loop

An SPL does not need to be a frontier AI researcher, but they need to understand how expert data, evals, human feedback, RLHF, benchmarks, and rubrics fit together.

Interview framing:

> I understand the SPL role as owning the operational layer between frontier AI labs and expert contributors. The lab identifies a model weakness, and the SPL helps translate that into the right expert workflow, quality standard, and delivery process.

### Translating Vague Needs Into Structured Work

Labs may not always come with perfectly scoped instructions. "We need better data for finance reasoning" has to become something more precise:

- Is this investment banking, equity research, accounting, forecasting, risk, or compliance?
- Are experts writing answers, reviewing outputs, ranking responses, or creating evals?
- What does "good" mean: accuracy, reasoning, compliance, clarity, or consistency?

This is very PM-like: convert ambiguity into scope, requirements, and an operating plan.

### Expert Sourcing And Selection

The SPL has to identify the right expert profile, not just fill a generic role.

For backend debugging, the need may be:

- senior backend engineers
- distributed systems experience
- strong code review judgment
- ability to explain why one model output is better than another

This maps well to my Interview Kickstart experience, where I worked with senior engineers and helped build expert-led course and review workflows.

### Workflow Design

Once experts are selected, the SPL designs the operating system for the work:

- task instructions
- rubrics
- expert onboarding
- review layers
- assignment structure
- escalation paths
- timelines
- quality checks
- throughput tracking

This is where the role becomes more than coordination. The SPL has to make expert judgment repeatable, measurable, and useful to the lab.

### Quality Control

Quality is one of the most important parts of the role because bad data can make the model worse or reduce client trust.

The SPL needs to ensure:

- experts understand the task
- reviewers apply rubrics consistently
- low-quality work is caught early
- disagreements are resolved
- final data is accurate, consistent, and useful

This requires judgment, not just process management.

### Client Communication

The SPL needs to communicate clearly with AI lab teams or internal customer-facing teams. They should be able to explain what is working, where quality is failing, whether timelines are realistic, what expert profiles are needed, and what tradeoffs exist between speed, cost, and quality.

The communication style has to be crisp, precise, and low-fluff.

### Analytical Problem-Solving

If a project is behind schedule, the weak answer is:

> We need more experts.

The stronger SPL diagnosis asks what the real bottleneck is:

- expert supply
- unclear instructions
- high rejection rate
- slow QA
- inconsistent rubric interpretation
- client-side ambiguity
- poor task design

This is the same root-cause thinking I have used in PM roles: do not treat the symptom as the problem.

### Speed And Ownership

The role seems intense because frontier-lab projects move fast. An SPL has to be comfortable with ambiguity, fast sourcing, changing client requirements, tight deadlines, high standards, messy operations, and owning outcomes without waiting for perfect instructions.

My takeaway: the SPL is the operator of the expert-data layer. The AI lab knows what capability it wants to improve. Experts have the domain judgment. The SPL makes the system between them work.
