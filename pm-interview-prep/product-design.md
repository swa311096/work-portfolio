# Product Design

Study notes for **product design** PM interview questions — how to structure answers, a worked example (“better gym experience”), and pitfalls. Deeper courses and drills: [TryExponent](https://www.tryexponent.com) (linked below).

_Tie practice answers to [Projects]({{ '/projects/' | relative_url }}) teardowns and [Work]({{ '/work/' | relative_url }}) stories where you shipped or improved UX._

---

Product design questions ask you to design or improve a product. Common question types include:

- **Design a product**: “Design an app for job-seekers to match with employers.”
- **Improve a product**: “Improve Headspace.”
- **Design for a specific audience**: “Design a vending machine for the blind.”
- **“Moonshots”**: A Google favorite — “Design X, assuming you have infinite resources.”

Overall, your goal is to come up with a **feasible** product given the constraints of the question. Feasible means your product solves a problem in a way that makes sense for the company and that users will like.

These typically take **25 minutes** excluding follow-ups. Interviewers often ask product design questions early to filter for essential product skills; they are also common on-site. Expect several in a standard PM loop.

## A framework for answering product design questions

A solid process is to first clarify the problem, gather relevant context until you understand users and their challenges, explore and select the best option, and finally justify your answer. Follow this **7-step** process:

1. Clarify and get context  
2. Define users  
3. Identify pain points and opportunity areas  
4. Brainstorm possible solutions  
5. Define a product vision  
6. Prioritize features  
7. Evaluate and recap

Below is a worked example for **“Design a better gym experience.”**

## Step 1: Clarify and get context

First, clarify any vagueness about the problem. After you’ve clarified ambiguous company names and keywords, gather context that helps you understand the problem space and any strategic considerations that might influence your design.

Questions like “What’s the timeline?” or “Are there any constraints I should be aware of?” are fair game, but be prepared for vague answers. If so, make **assumptions** and state them so the interviewer can correct you.

### Example

For **“Design a better gym experience”**, you might ask:

- “What do you mean by *gym experience*?”
- “What is *better*? Are there specific metrics we’re trying to drive?”

To align on context:

- “Am I improving a home gym product or a commercial gym?”
- “One location or a chain?”
- “A specific gym like Planet Fitness, or general improvements?”

Suppose the interviewer says: *“Make whatever assumptions you like; we just know that the gym experience works for some and not others and we want to make it better for more users.”*

You might respond that you’ll assume a **generic commercial gym** going forward.

![Product design whiteboard — clarify](https://images.ctfassets.net/x78yjrjc11pq/1GwR9KISVFJGHHYBkhFDFj/e66d68f1b8b50da8d6783e3d5cfd692f/How_to_Answer_Product_Design_Questions_Whiteboard_1.png)

## Step 2: Define users

Define subsets of users that **partition** the total user base, then pick an interesting subset for a deep dive. Ways to segment:

- Demographics (e.g. age, income)  
- Behavioral characteristics (frequency of use, certain actions)  
- Geographic location  

Segment in a way that is meaningful **for the question**. A strong answer often contrasts users with **very different needs**. Then pick a segment and say why it’s worth focusing on—e.g. strategic importance, scale of impact / depth of pain, and whether you (or the company in the prompt) can realistically serve them.

### Example

For **“Design a better gym experience”**, segment by **usage**:

- “Pro” users  
- Novice users  
- Users who only take classes  
- Users who have memberships but **don’t actually go**  

Suppose you choose **absent members**:

*“Users who have memberships but don’t go stand out: they were motivated enough to join and pay, but something blocks attendance. Fixing that could help other gym-goers and attract new members.”*

![Product design whiteboard — users](https://images.ctfassets.net/x78yjrjc11pq/2ZcCFM5hYf5mVBlZGkLgK4/bbd0d4cd41984279199fe257245fdcaa/How_to_Answer_Product_Design_Questions_Whiteboard_2.png)

## Step 3: Identify pain points and opportunity areas

Consider user **goals**. Explicit obstacles are **pain points**. If nothing blocks the goal but friction exists, that’s an **opportunity area**. Summarize goals, then brainstorm pains and opportunities.

**Tip:** use a “**broad then deep**” pass to narrow to the most important pain points — see [Exponent: broad then deep](https://www.tryexponent.com/courses/pm/general-interview/broad-then-deep).

Consider urgency, importance of decisions, and user circumstances. Say your reasoning out loud; it shows empathy.

### Example

Absent members likely wanted, in some mix:

- Better health  
- Look better  
- Confidence  
- Social connection  

What stops them? Emotional factors (e.g. uncomfortable, intimidated, bored) and logistical factors (busy schedules, traffic, inconvenient location).

Suppose you **prioritize emotional hurdles** over logistics (logistics vary heavily by user). You double down on **boredom** — especially **waiting for equipment at peak times**.

*“Users may skip the gym due to discomfort: sterile, intimidating spaces block confidence or social goals. Boredom matters too—especially waiting between sets during peak hours. Combined with a stressful day, the ‘should I go?’ decision gets harder. I’ll focus on boredom at the gym, specifically **waiting for popular equipment**.”*

![Product design whiteboard — pain points](https://images.ctfassets.net/x78yjrjc11pq/haQemJtxVKRERhlgUEIcu/e155971e40057f42325d386c9d1cd9ff/How_to_Answer_Product_Design_Questions_Whiteboard_3.png)

## Step 4: Brainstorm possible solutions

Brainstorm ideas that solve the pain you chose.

**Tip:** see [Exponent: brainstorm product ideas](https://www.tryexponent.com/courses/pm/pm-product-design/brainstorm-product-ideas).

Pull analogies from products you like that solve a **similar shape** of problem in another context (e.g. Duolingo for motivation, routine, and low-friction improvement).

Aim for **at least three** solid ideas before moving on; you can prune a longer list. Check each idea against the pain. Interviewers want energy and product passion.

### Example

Pain: bored while **waiting** at peak times. Ideas might include:

- Personalized workout recommendations that **route around** peak hours / busy equipment  
- **Gamification** to make the gym feel more engaging  
- A **between-sets activity** so wait time feels useful and less awkward  

![Product design whiteboard — solutions](https://images.ctfassets.net/x78yjrjc11pq/N0pmuNS5D6o2WqXWZxXpD/f38a4f151e25f3fff4fbae24b1a883be/How_to_Answer_Product_Design_Interview_Whiteboard_4.png)

## Step 5: Come up with a forward-looking product vision

Pick the strongest solution and briefly envision it **5–10 years** out. Add a **memorable tagline** (a soundbite for scoring). Put it on the whiteboard if you have one and refer back to it.

### Example

*"A gym visit is more than active workout time; non-active time is often awkward or boring. In a futuristic gym, that time could be fun and exciting."*

Add detail if useful:

*"Let’s build a gym with **engaging between-set experiences** so users stay in the right mindset and get more effective workouts."*

![Product design whiteboard — vision](https://images.ctfassets.net/x78yjrjc11pq/1a12tQ7CfKbKahSOpuWHNq/a304e7ac2da9e8eb5a166f5ab045b1e7/How_to_Answer_Product_Design_Interview_Whiteboard_5.png)

## Step 6: Prioritize features

Walk through a **short user journey** so your product sits in a real flow and stays user-centric, not “your favorite idea.”

Brainstorm features; prioritize by fit to the vision and pain. Useful dimensions:

- **Scale:** how many users does this help?  
- **Ease of expansion:** other segments, acquisition  
- **Strategic impact:** company vision / strategy  

You don’t need every pixel—do show **what users see and do** and how that delivers the vision.

### Example

Vision: *“Supporting activities between sets so users stay in the right mindset and have more effective workouts.”*

Detail the wait: *between machines* vs *between sets*. Only **5–10 minutes**; engagement without **pulling focus** from the workout.

*“We could ship an app: log workouts, stats, journal prompts, or **tips for the next set**—short, workout-adjacent, not distracting.”*

![Product design whiteboard — prioritize](https://images.ctfassets.net/x78yjrjc11pq/2ftkOyu51jKcRBvBVeW04B/6f85a1ca6ce9bbfae1febc141fe84f38/How_to_Answer_Product_Design_Interview_Whiteboard_6.png)

## Step 7: Evaluate and recap

Summarize insights and your design, then **evaluate**:

- **Tradeoffs** you made  
- Alternate paths and **edge cases**  
- What you’d do with **more time**  

Expect: *“Risks?”* and *“Implementation challenges?”* Addressing these preemptively shows grounding in reality.

### Example

*"Risks: gyms are busy with heavy equipment—**too much distraction can be unsafe**. We must avoid **slowing workouts** or **worsening bottlenecks**."*

## Tips for answering product design questions

### Consider company strategy

- **Mission:** why does the company care? How does this product support it?  
- **Strategy:** relevant goals; new opportunities  
- **Market:** alternatives, gaps, what’s valuable  

### Ace moonshot questions by abstracting the problem

Moonshots ask for bold, transformative answers—often associated with Google-style prompts.

You’re on track if you solve the problem **completely and simply** for the user, ideally removing root causes.

Two practice strategies:

1. **Black box:** input = user’s current state; output = desired state. Strip assumptions about *how* to bridge the gap.  
2. **[5 Whys](https://www.mindtools.com/a3mi00v/5-whys)** until you hit a root problem.  

Example: “Alarm clock for deaf users” → goal is **wake on time**, not “tweak audio alarms.” Reframe around the goal.

### Improve an existing product

For “Improve Instagram’s home tab”–style questions, account for **current users**, **product goals**, and **market dynamics**. Common angles: under-served users, new use cases, strategic expansion.

## Common pitfalls

- **Jumping straight to a solution** — show reasoning; don’t lock in your first idea.  
- **Forgetting to segment users** — even with “Gmail for kids,” *kids* is broad; narrow age/school level and constraints (literacy, safety, tech skill).  
- **Designing for everybody** — scope collapse; weak insights.  
- **Treating the framework as a checklist** — use steps to find insight; stay holistic.
